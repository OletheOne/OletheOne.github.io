How to Use This Guide

Read Once · Save Hours

Two halves: a strict ordered Route, and the Reference Files it links into. Here's the reading contract.

The Route (Phases I–VII) is a strict, ordered walkthrough: it tells you what to do on day one, when to buy each property, when to recruit each dealer, when to switch products, and when to pick a fight with the cartel. The Reference Files are deep dives into each system — mixing, dealers, properties, employees, money, police. The Route links into them at the exact moment each system unlocks, so you never read a wall of theory before you need it.

  • Follow the phases in order. Each phase ends with exit criteria. Don't advance until you meet them — Schedule I punishes over-expansion far more than it punishes patience.
  • Check off the checklists. They're interactive and cover every purchase and unlock that matters. Anything not on a list is optional flavor.
  • Read every amber WARNING. There are a handful of ways to genuinely stall a save in this game (mostly around the cartel and cash limits). All of them are flagged.
  • Green PAYDAY callouts mark disproportionately profitable moves. If you only skim, skim for those.

Operation Primer

Core Systems

The five systems that drive everything: time, XP, money, customers, product.

The Day Cycle

One in-game day runs from 7:00 AM to 4:00 AM, at roughly one real minute per in-game hour. At 4:00 AM the clock freezes until you sleep — this frozen window is the most valuable time in the game. Curfew starts at 9:00 PM (police will stop and search you outside), and sleeping ends the day, pays employees, processes rent and orders, and banks your XP into rank progress.

Payday · The 4 AM Rule

Time stops at 4:00 AM but you don't. Gas-Marts stay open, machines keep working when interacted with, and nothing costs you daylight. Do all your mixing experiments, restocking, base reorganization, and long drives during the freeze. Veterans do practically all logistics at 4 AM and spend the actual day selling and managing. Make this a habit in Phase I and every later phase gets faster.

XP & Ranks

Everything meaningful is gated behind rank: store items, regions, suppliers, and gear. You climb from Street Rat through Hoodlum, Peddler, Hustler, Bagman, Enforcer, Shot Caller, Block Boss, Underlord, Baron, and finally Kingpin — each rank has five tiers. XP comes from harvesting plants, completing deals (yours and your dealers'), and escaping the police. Customer daily spending limits also scale with your level, so ranking up literally makes every customer richer.

Dirty Money vs. Clean Money

Sales pay in cash (dirty). Your bank balance (clean) is what buys properties, businesses, vehicles, and most big-ticket items. You convert dirty to clean two ways: deposit up to $10,000 per week at ATMs, or launder through businesses you own, each of which cleans a daily cap. Cash flow is never your problem in this game — cleaning it is. That's why this guide has you buying laundering businesses far earlier than feels natural.

Customers

Every named NPC in a region is a potential customer. You unlock them by offering a free sample (or by being introduced through their friends once your mutual contacts like you). Each customer has: a product affinity (weed / meth / coke / shrooms), quality standards, favorite effects, a daily spending limit tied to your level, and a relationship meter. Successful, on-time deals raise relationship and addiction; both raise what they'll pay. Customers pay a quality bonus of +15% per quality tier above their standard, and maxed-relationship customers tolerate markups of roughly 30–60% over list price.

How a sample mechanically works: walk up to the NPC, open the dialogue, choose Offer Free Sample, and hand over one packaged unit from your hotbar (a baggie is fine). They wander off to try it and, if it lands, text you within about a day — from then on they're a contact in your phone with a full customer file you can read. Samples fail for two reasons: product below the NPC's quality standard, or a product type they have no affinity for. In Phase I everyone around the motel tolerates decent weed, so don't overthink it; from Westville onward, check the district's leanings before burning stock on a sweep.

Product

Four product lines, unlocked in order: weed (grown), meth (cooked from purchased ingredients), cocaine (grown then processed), and shrooms (grown, post-Shrooms update). Raw product is worth little; the money is in mixing — layering up to eight effects onto a base using absurd household ingredients. A $35 batch of weed becomes a $150+ product with four cheap mixes. The Mixing Lab reference file is the single most valuable page in this guide.

Guiding Principle

Schedule I is a logistics game wearing a crime-sim costume. The player who wins is not the one who sells the most — it's the one who automates production, delegates sales to dealers, launders on a schedule, and spends their own time only on the three things NPCs can't do: unlocking customers, expanding territory, and fighting the cartel. Every phase in this route pushes you toward that end state.

Phase I — The RV & Motel Days

Street Rat I–III

Finish the prologue, squeeze the RV dry, sell your first batches by hand, and bank ~$1,500.

The Prologue, Done Right

The game opens with your RV lab going up in flames and Uncle Nelson setting you up in Hyland Point's motel with a grow tent and a handful of OG Kush seeds. Play the prologue straight — it's a tutorial — but with one crucial exception:

Warning · Loot the RV First

Before you answer Uncle Nelson's payphone call for the first time, strip everything you can carry out of the RV. The starting items inside (supplies, packaging, loose cash) are free capital, and the prologue moves on without you if you rush the phone. Thirty seconds of looting here is your seed money.

Your First Grows

Your motel room fits a grow tent. The loop: buy soil and OG Kush seeds (a seed runs about $30; basic soil is ~$10 and lasts one grow, long-life soil is ~$30 and lasts three — switch to long-life the moment you can float it), plant, water, wait (a plant matures in about a day and yields roughly 8 buds), harvest, bag every bud into a baggie (~$1 each) at the packaging station — customers never buy loose product. Each raw bud baggie sells for roughly $35–40. Do not sell raw for long — the moment the game hands you a mixing station quest in Phase II, raw sales are obsolete.

  • Water on a rhythm: soil shows a moisture level and needs topping up two or three times per grow — make morning and evening checks a fixed habit. A plant that dries out completely dies, and dead plants are pure loss.
  • Buy additives once you have ~$50 spare. The hardware store sells three: Fertilizer (raises harvest quality — a direct price bump, since customers pay +15% per quality tier), Speed Grow (shorter grow), and PGR (bigger yield). Fertilizer first; quality compounds through every sale you'll ever make.
  • Sell everything you grow, in person. Walk Northtown, approach NPCs, offer product. The deal screen lets you set quantity and price: open at about 20% over listed value. If they counter with a lowball, counter once more just above list — they accept far more often than the tone of the haggle suggests — but take any offer at or above list rather than pushing a third time and risking a walk-away. Early direct deals are your XP engine (about 20 XP per sale), and each successful sale warms that customer up.
  • Offer free samples liberally. A sample converts an NPC into a repeat customer who texts you orders. Start with the NPCs clustered around the motel and the central strip — Austin Steiner, Kyle Cooley, Mick Lubbin, Peggy Myers, and Jessi Waters are classic first unlocks, and once someone likes you they'll introduce you to their friends (Kyle leads to the Cooley family, for instance). Unlocked customers are permanent infrastructure; product given away now returns tenfold.
  • Read each customer's file. Once unlocked, a customer's entry in your phone shows their preferred drug type, quality standard, favorite effects, and spending limit. Check it before every deal — it's also how you'll pick dealer assignments in Phase II.
  • Meet deal windows. When a customer texts an order, the text names a product, quantity, price, meeting spot, and time window (e.g. "6 PM–8 PM by the basketball court"). From the messages app you can accept, counter-offer (different product, quantity, or price — countering with your mixed product at a higher price is routine and usually accepted), or decline. Once accepted, the deal pins to your map; be standing there with the packaged product in your inventory inside the window. Arriving late or empty-handed craters the relationship — declining costs far less than failing, so never accept a deal you can't physically make.

Free Money on the Ground

Payday · Trash Runs

Buy a Trash Grabber (~$20) from the hardware store — it picks trash in one click instead of a crouch-and-grab, and you'll make the cost back in one lap. Hyland Point is covered in litter, and Cash for Trash machines (bright green kiosks; there's one on the Northtown main strip near the Gas-Mart, and each region adds more) pay a few dollars per piece fed in. The route that works: grab a full inventory of litter on the walk between your grow checks and deals, and empty it into whichever machine you pass. It's small money but it's clean-adjacent early cash, it tidies your surroundings (trash buildup hurts performance over long saves), and 500 pieces earns the Upstanding Citizen achievement along the way.

Phase II — Sweatshop & First Dealer

Street Rat IV → Hoodlum I

Rent real production space, unlock mixing, recruit Benji, buy your first laundering business.

Move Out of the Motel

The motel room is a shoebox. As soon as you have the cash, rent the Sweatshop (~$800 down + ~$100/day rent) — it's the run-down unit in Northtown with a "For Lease" sign; walk up to the sign and confirm to rent (this one takes cash, unlike later properties that need clean bank money). Rent is deducted automatically when you sleep, so keep a float in hand.

Set it up as a dense weed floor. Concretely, the space comfortably fits: 4 grow tents (or pots once you can afford grow lights), 1 packaging station, 1 mixing station, 2–3 storage shelves for soil/seeds/ingredients, and a bed for yourself. Buy it all from the hardware store during a 4 AM run and place it via build mode (the furniture icon on your phone). Keep the walking path from tents → packaging → door clear; you'll do this circuit dozens of times a day.

The main event: unlock mixing

Key Unlock · The Mixing Station · ~$500

The Moment Schedule I Actually Begins

The main quest line has you buy a Mixing Station from the hardware store. Mixing lets you layer effects onto product with cheap consumables — a banana, a cucumber-brand soda, motor oil — and every effect multiplies sale price. From this point on, never sell unmixed product.

Operating it is simple once you've done it once: put unpackaged buds in the product slot (packaged product won't mix — bag after mixing, always), one ingredient in the ingredient slot, pull the handle, wait a few seconds, take the new product out. To stack effects, feed that output straight back in with the next ingredient. Start with this cheap early chain on OG Kush (all three ingredients are on the Gas-Mart shelves — Cuke ~$2, Banana ~$2, Gasoline ~$5):

OG Kush → Cuke → Banana → Gasoline → sells ~2.5–3× the raw price for pennies of ingredients

One warning while you're on Mk1 stations: each mix spits out a piece of trash. Keep a bin next to the mixer or your floor becomes a landfill.

Full recipe theory, the effect system, and the exact chains to graduate into are in Reference A: The Mixing Lab. Read the "How Mixing Works" half of that file now; come back to the premium recipes in Phase IV+.

Recruit Benji

Key Recruit · First Dealer · $500 · Motel Room 2

Benji Coleman

Keep following the quest line and unlocking customers; once you've built a base of unlocked Northtown contacts, the quest points you at Benji Coleman in Motel Room 2 — the room next to your old one. Knock on his door, pay the $500 recruitment fee in cash, and he's yours. He autonomously sells to customers you assign him, for a 20% cut, while you do literally anything else. To assign customers: open his entry in your phone's contacts, tap Assign Customers, and pick from your unlocked list — you can reassign freely later, so nothing here is permanent.

  • Which customers to assign (he handles up to 8): give Benji your established, routine Northtown customers — the ones whose relationship you've already built and who text small, frequent orders. The Phase I unlocks near his motel (Austin Steiner, Kyle Cooley, Mick Lubbin, Peggy Myers, Jessi Waters, and the like) are ideal: they're clustered around his home base, so he never misses a deal window walking to them. Keep 2–3 customers for yourself — your highest spenders and any customer whose relationship isn't maxed yet. Personal deals pay double the XP, take no 20% cut, and are the only way to push markup and grow a relationship quickly; dealers maintain relationships, they don't build them well.
  • What to stock him with: your standard mixed weed — the Cuke → Banana → Gasoline Kush from this phase. Product choice absolutely matters: customers buy their preferred drug type first (check each assigned customer's file), and Northtown at this stage is weed country, so one product covers everyone. It also has to meet their quality standards — a dealer holding schwag that his assigned customers won't accept earns nothing. Don't waste your best premium batches on him, though: dealers sell at a fair auto-calculated price and take their cut, so top-shelf product always earns more sold personally.
  • Keep him stocked. Visit him, hand over mixed product in bulk — 15–20 units lasts him about a day at full assignment. A dealer with an empty inventory earns nothing — restocking dealers becomes a fixed part of your daily loop from now until Kingpin.
  • Dealer sales still earn you ~10 XP each. Delegation doesn't slow your rank climb; it multiplies it.

Dealer mechanics, all recruits, costs, and optimal customer assignments: Reference B: The Dealer Network.

Buy the Laundromat

Payday · Launder Early

Your first business purchase should come shockingly early: the Laundromat (~$4,000, cleans ~$500/day). It's a storefront in Northtown with a "For Sale" sign out front — buy it at the sign like a property. The weekly ATM deposit cap (~$10,000) throttles clean money, and clean money is what buys every property from here forward. Every day the Laundromat isn't cleaning $500 is a day your empire's growth is capped. Buy it before you buy comfort upgrades — before a better car, before decor, before anything. How to actually run laundering once you own it is a two-step ritual covered in Reference E; the short version is you load dirty cash at the business's counter and it arrives in your bank when you sleep.

Phase III — Westville & the Cook

Hoodlum → Peddler

Open Westville, start the meth line, upgrade housing, hire your first employees.

Open Westville

Hoodlum rank unlocks Westville, the region west of Northtown. Day one in a new region is always the same drill, and you'll repeat it for every region in the game:

  1. Sample sweep. Walk the district with a stack of your best mixed weed and offer free samples to every NPC. Each new customer permanently raises regional demand.
  2. Meet the vendors. Westville adds shops with new mixing ingredients and gear.
  3. Note the dead drops and layout — you'll be routing dealers and deliveries through here for the rest of the game.
Warning · Cartel Rules Apply to New Regions

If you're playing post-Cartel update, the Benzies family contests territory from Westville onward, and two early choices can waste permanent progress: graffiti tags sprayed before you're hostile with the cartel count for nothing, and unlocking every customer in a region can stop cartel dealers from spawning — removing your best influence-farming target. The safe rule for every new region: unlock ~5 customers, no more, and spray no graffiti until you have read Phase VI and gone hostile. Phase VI covers the whole war; this warning exists so you don't disarm yourself before it starts.

The main event: start the meth line

Key Unlock · Product Line 2 · Westville

Meth — Your First Big Margin Jump

  1. Give free samples to Westville locals (Jerry Montero and Meg Cooley are the classic targets) until one befriends you and introduces you to Shirley Watts, the pseudo supplier. Shirley works like all suppliers: text her from your phone, pick items and quantities from her list (Pseudo, Acid, and Phosphorus — everything a cook needs comes from her), and the cost goes onto her debt ledger rather than being paid up front. The goods appear in a dead drop — a marked stash spot (dumpster/crate) pinned on your map — usually by the next morning, so place orders during the 4 AM freeze and collect on tomorrow's circuit. Pay the debt down from the same text thread; let it hit her limit and she stops filling orders, so clear it every visit.
  2. Buy a Chemistry Station (~$1,000) and Lab Oven (~$1,000) from Oscar at the warehouse and place them at your property.
  3. The loop: load 1 Pseudo + 1 Acid + 1 Phosphorus into the Chemistry Station and start it (~8 in-game hours — start it in the evening so it finishes overnight) → collect the liquid meth → into the Lab Oven (~6 hours) → ~10 units of crystal → mix, then package.

The margins are why this is the main event: one cook consumes one of each ingredient — about $120–140 all-in — and the oven turns the batch into ~10 units of crystal. Unmixed crystal already sells around $70 (that's ~$700 back on a ~$130 cook before you've mixed anything); mixed meth clears well over $100 per unit with the same cheap Gas-Mart chains you used on Kush. Meth customers skew Westville/Downtown — check affinities before flooding Northtown weed loyalists with crystal. From now on run both lines in parallel: weed for the loyal base, meth for margins.

Upgrade to the Bungalow, hire your first staff

Buy the Bungalow (~$6,000, Westville) when clean cash allows — it's paid from your bank balance at the for-sale sign, which is exactly why the Laundromat came first. It's your first property big enough for a real workforce. Hire from the ledger at your property:

  • 1 Botanist (~$100/day): assign to your pots — seeds in, watering, harvest to drying racks or shelves. Your growing is now automated.
  • 1 Chemist (~$150/day): assign to the Chemistry Station, Lab Oven, and mixing stations. Your cooking is now automated.
  • 1 Handler (~$100/day): moves items between stations/shelves on routes you define, and can package.

The hire isn't done when they walk in the door — three setup steps, in order, or the employee stands idle: pay the one-time signing fee (roughly $1,000–2,000 by role), then use the management clipboard to assign them a bed (buy beds first; no bed, no work), then assign their workstations — the botanist to specific pots, the chemist to specific stations, the handler to specific shelf→station routes. Finally drop several days of wages into the on-site briefcase/locker — wages draw from it every night, and an unpaid employee simply stops. Full ratios, wages, and routing logic: Reference D: Payroll & Automation.

Also make sure the employee has supplies to work with: a botanist needs seeds and soil sitting on a shelf they can reach, a chemist needs ingredients shelved next to the station. Automation in Schedule I fails silently — the single most useful debugging habit is walking the floor once a day and asking "what is each person waiting on?"

Also this phase: buy your remaining laundering businesses as cash allows — Taco Ticklers, the Car Wash, and the Post Office each add daily cleaning capacity (see Reference E: Money & Laundering). Once you have 5+ Westville customers texting orders, recruit your second dealer, Molly Presley (~$1,000) — like every dealer she's recruited at her home spot in her region: walk up, pay the fee in cash, then assign her your Westville customers from her contact entry. Stock her with mixed meth, not weed, since her Westville book skews meth-affinity (see Reference B for the full assignment doctrine). Grab a cheap vehicle from the dealership (even the ~$2,000 beater) — trunk space turns restock runs from three trips into one, and a trunk full of product doesn't show up in a curfew stop-and-search the way your pockets do.

Phase IV — Downtown & the Barn

Peddler → Hustler

Open Downtown, buy the Barn, hit Peddler V for the Brick Press, and convert from hustler to factory owner.

Open Downtown

Peddler unlocks Downtown — richer customers, new vendors, and the pawn shop. Run the standard region drill (samples, vendors, layout), respecting the 5-customer cartel rule. Downtown customers favor stronger effect profiles and tolerate higher prices; this is where your mixed meth line shines.

The main event: the Barn

Key Property · ~$25,000 · The Road Out of Town

The Barn — Your Factory

The best value property in the game and the backbone of your mid-game: two floors of open space that fit a complete, closed production loop. The proven layout:

  • Grow floor: 16 pots (upgrade from tents to individual pots with grow lights — better yields), 4+ drying racks. Dried buds are a full quality tier up; never skip racks again.
  • Process line: 2+ Chemistry Stations, 2+ Lab Ovens, and a daisy chain of 4–8 Mixing Station Mk2s — each mixer's output routed to the next, each pre-loaded with one recipe ingredient, so raw product enters mixer 1 and finished premium product exits the last one untouched by human hands. The chain is the recipe, one step per station: for the 8-step meth chain from Reference A, that's mixer 1 = Banana, 2 = Cuke, 3 = Paracetamol, 4 = Gasoline, 5 = Cuke, 6 = Battery, 7 = Horse Semen, 8 = Mega Bean. To wire it: open the management clipboard, select each mixer, and set its output destination to the next mixer in line (the last one outputs to the packaging/brick shelf). Then give each Handler a route from the ingredient shelf to the 2–4 mixers he keeps loaded, and assign Chemists to operate the stations. Physically place the mixers in recipe order along a wall so a glance tells you which step a stalled batch died at.
  • Logistics: large storage shelves for every input, packaging stations, employee beds upstairs, bins for trash.
  • Staff: work toward 2–3 Botanists, 2–3 Chemists, 2 Handlers, 1 Cleaner — approaching the ~10-employee cap per property.
Payday · The Mk2 Mixer & The Daisy Chain

Buy Mixing Station Mk2s (~$2,000, from Oscar) the moment they're available. They auto-insert ingredients, hold stacks of 20, run faster, and produce no trash. A daisy chain of Mk2s staffed by chemists is the single highest-leverage structure in Schedule I: it converts a ~$40 stack of ingredients into thousands of dollars of product per cycle with zero player time. This is the machine that pays for everything in Phases V–VII.

Peddler V: the Brick Press

At Peddler V you unlock the Brick Press (buy from Oscar, place it at the end of your mixer chain). Load it with 20 unpackaged units of the same product and press — out comes a single brick that stacks, carries, and hands over as one item. Bricks are how you restock dealers in one handoff instead of twenty, and how bulk deals get done. From here on the packaging split is fixed: dealers get bricks of your volume mix; your premium product gets baggies/jars for personal high-margin sales — bricks are for bulk, not for pushing markup.

Round out the network

  • Dealers: recruit your third dealer, Brad Crosby (~$2,000, Downtown), as Downtown customers stack up. Downtown's mid-tier spenders lean meth and sedative-profile mixes, so brick him up accordingly; keep every dealer at 8 assigned customers near their home turf (Reference B).
  • Businesses: by end of Phase IV, own all four laundering businesses. That's roughly $2,000+/day of cleaning — ~$140k/week of legitimate purchasing power (Reference E).
  • Vehicles: upgrade to the van; it's a rolling warehouse for restock circuits.

Phase V — The Docks & Coke

Hustler → Bagman

Open the Docks, unlock cocaine, buy the Docks Warehouse, and split production across two industrial sites.

The main event: unlock cocaine

Key Unlock · Product Line 3 · The Docks

Cocaine — Your Endgame Product

Hustler unlocks the Docks. Run the region drill, and prioritize unlocking Docks customers because befriending them is what gets you introduced to Salvador Moreno — the coca seed supplier. He works exactly like Shirley: text your order, cost goes on his debt ledger, collect seeds and gear from the dead drop, keep the debt paid down or he cuts you off.

The cocaine loop: plant coca seeds in your pots (grow like weed but slower — budget about two days per grow — so dedicate a bank of pots rather than rotating them with weed) → harvest leaves → Cauldron (~$3,000 from Oscar) cooks a batch of about 20 leaves plus a can of gasoline (~6 in-game hours) into paste → Lab Oven finishes the paste into cocaine → mix → package → sell. Chemists can run both cauldron and oven, so the whole line automates the same way meth did.

Base coke sells around $150; premium mixed coke clears $500–700+ per unit.

The Docks Warehouse & the two-site split

Buy the Docks Warehouse (~$50,000) — the biggest floor plan in the game. Because each property caps at ~10 employees, the optimal endgame is a two-site split:

  • Barn = agriculture. Max pots of coca (and a weed corner), drying racks, botanist-heavy staff. Output: dried leaves to shelves.
  • Docks Warehouse = industry. Cauldrons, Lab Ovens, the grand Mk2 mixer chain, Brick Press, packaging, chemist/handler-heavy staff. Input: leaves you shuttle from the Barn by van.
  • The van run between them is your one daily manual logistics task — do it at 4 AM (a road barricade near the Docks clears late at night, making the drive trivial).
Note · Don't Overbuild

A 16-pot coca floor already covers every buyer in the game once mixed. Eighty-pot megafarms look impressive but outrun demand and your laundering caps. Build to what your customer base consumes, then spend the surplus on the cartel war and net-worth goals instead.

Meanwhile: keep the machine turning

  • Recruit your fourth dealer, Jane Lucero (~$3,000, Docks), as Docks customers stack up. The Docks is where coke affinity starts, so split her bricks between mixed coke and your meth volume line; Reference B lists ideal turf assignments.
  • Switch your personal selling entirely to premium coke deals; let dealers move the weed/meth volume in bricks.
  • Shrooms (if your version has the Shrooms update) slot in as a fourth line grown like weed; the same premium mix chain that caps coke also caps shrooms at ~$300+/unit. Optional, but the Docks Warehouse has room.
  • Start converting surplus clean money at the pawn shop/boutique into gold bars — the endgame store of value (Reference E explains the sell-day trick).

Phase VI — The Cartel War

Bagman → Enforcer+

Break the Benzies family's grip region by region and clear the influence gates blocking Suburbia and Uptown.

The setup: truce or war

Early in a post-Cartel-update save, Thomas Benzies offers you a deal: a truce where you deliver product to the mansion at insulting prices and, in exchange, his people leave you alone. Here's the catch that decides everything:

Warning · The Truce Is a Progress Cap

You cannot progress past Westville while the truce holds. The truce is training wheels for your early phases at best. Take it if you want peace while you build Phases II–III, but you must break it (text T.B., or just stop delivering) to advance — and once you go hostile, it is irreversible. The recommended line: accept the truce early if offered, break it deliberately at the start of Phase IV, and treat everything below as the playbook from that moment on. On older saves the game sets you hostile by default and skips the choice entirely.

Cartel dealers briefly step outside their safehouse door on a daily reset tick (historically midnight; some patches have shifted the timing — camp the door around resets and confirm on your version). A daily circuit — check the safehouse at reset, knock out, kill, loot their pockets — is repeatable forever and pays twice: −200 influence and free product (including cocaine long before you can make it). Combat prep

Buy a bat and an M1911 with spare magazines from Stan at the illegal warehouse, and carry them always once hostile — ambushes trigger mid-deal with no warning. The dealer takedown that earns the −200 combo, step by step: approach from behind or the side (sprinting straight at them from the front starts a brawl on their terms), swing the bat at the head until they drop unconscious, loot them while they're down — crouch, aim at the back pocket — then finish them for the kill credit. Loot before the kill; bodies are gone on the next tick. Guns are for ambushes: when three thugs jump you, backpedal out of melee range and put them down with the M1911 rather than trading bat swings at 3-to-1. And check the minimap first, every time — doing any of this in a cop's sight line converts a −200 influence play into an arrest and a seizure.

Warning · Known Ways to Stall the War

1. Unlocking every customer in a region too early. No free customers = cartel dealers have no reason to leave the safehouse = your best influence farm goes dormant, and some players have been left camping a door that never opens. Leave a few customers unlocked per region until its influence gate is cleared, then finish the sweep.

The Kingpin grind

Everything unlocks by around Bagman/Enforcer; the ranks after that are pure XP scale, and Kingpin itself has deep repeatable tiers. The efficient close-out:

  • Volume is XP. Every dealer sale ticks ~10 XP; six stocked dealers plus your automated factories rank you passively. Harvests tick XP too — another reason the pots never sit empty.
  • Chase the net-worth ladder: $100k (Businessman) → $1M (Bigwig) → $10M (Magnate). Net worth counts on end-of-day, so sleep to bank milestones. Gold bars from the boutique are the compact way to store it.
  • Max your customer book. With all influence gates cleared, finish unlocking every remaining customer in every region — each is permanent passive income and starves any cartel remnant.
  • Buy the toys. The golden skateboard is the traditional "I've won" purchase (and an achievement).

Reference A — The Mixing Lab

~80% of Your Profit

The system that generates most of your money. Master it here.

How mixing works

  • Place an unpackaged product and one ingredient in a mixing station; ~6 seconds later you have a new product. Feed that product back in with the next ingredient to stack effects.
  • Products hold a maximum of 8 effects. Each effect carries a price multiplier; the sale price is base price × (1 + sum of multipliers).
  • Order is everything. Each ingredient adds its signature effect and transforms effects already present — Banana then Gasoline is not Gasoline then Banana. Follow chains exactly, left to right.
  • Some worthless effects are stepping stones: Paranoia adds nothing itself but converts into Anti-Gravity (one of the top multipliers) with the right follow-up. Trust the chain even when a middle step looks bad.
  • The Mk2 station (from Oscar) auto-inserts, holds 20-stacks, runs faster, makes no trash. Mandatory at scale.
  • Bag or jar the finished product — customers don't buy loose.
Note · Experimenting Without Pain

Test new chains during the 4 AM freeze (Gas-Mart is open 24/7 for ingredients), and lean on the community mix calculators (search "Schedule 1 calculator") to preview effects, price, and profit before you commit a real batch. Recipes below are community-verified staples; exact dollar values drift a few percent between patches.

Selling what you mixed

  • Match effects to districts. Broad tendencies: Northtown likes Energizing/Euphoric/Munchies, Westville likes Focused/Thought-Provoking, Downtown likes Sedating/Toxic/Shrinking, Docks likes Long-Faced/Refreshing/Anti-Gravity. A liked-effect match lets you push price harder.
  • Price to move. Start around list, push +30% on regulars, up to +60% on maxed-relationship addicts. A $500 bag that doesn't sell earns less than a $150 bag that does.
  • Quality pays. +15% per quality tier above the customer's standard — drying racks and good grow gear are direct price increases.
  • Save rare ingredients (Horse Semen, Mega Bean) for the premium chain only. Never burn them experimenting.

Which customers to assign

The mechanics first: recruit a dealer by walking to their home spot and paying the fee in cash, then open their entry in your phone's contacts and tap Assign Customers to pick up to 8 from your unlocked list. Assignments aren't permanent — reassign any customer in the same menu at any time, so rebalance freely as regions open. Assignment quality is the difference between a dealer clearing $2,000 a day and one clearing $400. Three rules, in priority order:

  • Cluster by geography first. Dealers walk to their deals. A customer three blocks from the dealer's base gets served on time, every time; a cross-district assignment means missed windows, and missed windows crater relationships you spent samples building. Open the map, look at where the customer lives and hangs out, and assign them to whichever dealer's turf that is — never the other way around.
  • Hand off matured relationships, keep developing ones. Dealers are maintenance, not growth: they'll keep a warm customer warm, but they build relationship far slower than your personal deals do, and they never push markup. The rhythm for every region is the same — you unlock a customer with a sample, run their first several deals personally until relationship is high, then hand them to the local dealer and move your attention to the next unlock. A freshly-sampled customer assigned straight to a dealer is money left on the table for weeks.
  • Keep the whales for yourself. Each region has a few standout spenders (they're obvious in the customer file — highest daily limits, lowest price sensitivity). Those are worth your personal time forever: no 20% cut, +30–60% markup on maxed relationships, and double XP. Everyone else goes to the network.

Which product to give them — and yes, it matters

It matters in three distinct ways, and getting it wrong is the most common silent leak in a dealer network:

  • Drug type must match the assigned customers. Every customer has a preferred product line (it's in their file: weed / meth / coke / shrooms). A dealer only fulfills an order if he's holding something the customer will buy — a meth-affinity customer served by a dealer carrying only weed is simply a lost sale, repeated daily. Before stocking a dealer, skim his 8 assignments: if six prefer meth and two prefer weed, his bricks should be mostly meth with a weed sidecar. As a shortcut, regional affinity is strong — Benji's Northtown book is weed country, Molly's Westville book skews meth, Jane's Docks book is where coke affinity starts, and Wei and Leo's endgame books want premium coke.
  • Quality must clear the customers' standards. Customers hold dealers to the same quality bar they hold you to. If a dealer's stock is below an assigned customer's standard, those deals fail or fizzle. Mid-game onward, everything that comes off your drying racks and Mk2 chain clears every bar in the game, so this mostly bites people who dump early low-grade surplus on a late-game dealer.
  • Don't give dealers your ceiling product. Dealers sell at a fair, auto-calculated price and take 20%. They will never extract the +60% markup a maxed-relationship addict pays you for an 8-effect premium bag. So run two tiers: a cheap, fast volume mix (a 3–4 step Gas-Mart chain on weed or meth) bricked up for the network, and the premium chain (Reference A's 8-step) bagged individually for your personal deals. The one exception is Leo Rivers: Uptown's spending limits are so high that even at a 20% cut he should carry premium coke.

Operating doctrine

  • 8 customers each, home turf only. Dealers walk to deals; cross-town assignments mean missed windows. Assign customers clustered around the dealer's base.
  • Restock in bricks, daily. Walk to the dealer (they're home outside deal hours), open the dialogue, and transfer product straight into their inventory grid. An empty dealer earns $0 and every missed customer order quietly feeds cartel dealers instead (post-Cartel update). The daily brick drop is non-negotiable. A full 8-customer book burns through roughly a brick (20 units) a day; drop two if you'll skip a day.
  • Collect their cash from the same screen when you restock; it piles up on them otherwise (and dealers holding fat stacks are juicier cartel targets).
  • They're targets. Hostile-cartel saves see Benzies thugs jump your dealers. Losses happen; keep them stocked anyway — their volume dwarfs the shrinkage.
  • You still get ~10 XP per dealer sale. A full six-dealer network is a passive rank engine.

  • The hiring sequence, in order: pay the signing fee at your property's hiring ledger → assign a bed (management clipboard) → assign workstations or routes (same clipboard) → stock wages in the briefcase → confirm supplies are on a shelf they can reach.   • Beds first. A hire without an assigned bed won't work. Beds upstairs at the Barn; along the walls at the Warehouse.
  • Watch the bottleneck, not the workers. If product piles up before one station type, add stations (or a chemist) there — the chain runs at the speed of its slowest link.

Gold bars & the pawn shop trick

Payday · The Endgame Piggy Bank

Once laundering outruns your spending, buy gold bars from the boutique (next to the real estate office) as your store of value. The pawn shop's buyback isn't random — its offer swings from roughly 44% to 180% of item value on a cycle keyed to the item and the day of the week. So: buy gold with surplus clean money, check the pawn offer daily, and sell only on the fat days. Played patiently, gold bars are a profit engine on top of a piggy bank, and they compress millions of net worth into a shelf.

Reference F — Heat & Police

Pay Zero Tax

The police are a tax on carelessness. Here's how to never pay it.

  • Curfew (9 PM–morning): cops stop and search anyone out at night. When one flags you down, you get a choice: comply (he searches your pockets — fine if you're clean) or run. Decide by what you're carrying, not by nerve: clean pockets, always comply and walk away in ten seconds; dirty pockets, run immediately — a search conviction costs more than any chase. Route night business through back streets, or simply do night logistics at 4 AM when the map is quiet, and daytime selling.
  • Searches only see your person. Product in your pockets or hands convicts you; product in a packed box you're carrying or in a vehicle trunk does not come up in a standard stop. So the transport doctrine: bricks and bulk go in the trunk, personal deal stock goes in as few pocket slots as possible, and you top up from the trunk between deals.
  • Wanted status: the arrest only happens if they catch you — so break line of sight first (duck through a building interior, hop a fence, round two corners), then leave the district and stay unseen until the wanted status times out. Sprinting in a straight road chase loses; cutting through interiors wins. Escaping a "Wanted: Dead or Alive" pursuit pays XP equal to several deals — experienced players deliberately farm escapes while making deliveries. Do this only once you know the map.
  • Arrest costs: every item of product and all dirty cash on your person is confiscated, plus a fine — then you're released to carry on. Stock at properties, money in the bank, and trunk contents are untouched, which is the whole logic of the doctrine above: an arrest should only ever cost you one deal's worth of pocket inventory.
  • Dealing in front of cops, tagging in sight lines, brawling in the open — all self-inflicted. Check the minimap before every deal and every cartel move.
  • Cops as a resource: post-Cartel, a downed officer reliably drops an M1911. Hostile-run players treat the occasional confrontation as an armory visit. High risk; your call.

Reference G — Field Notes & Pitfalls

Run Killers

Everything that quietly ruins runs, in one place.

  • Selling raw product past Phase II. Mixing multiplies price 3–8×. Every unmixed sale is a donation.
  • Wrong recipe order. The chain is a sequence, not a shopping list. One swapped step changes the whole effect stack.
  • Overpricing. Unsold product is worth exactly $0/day. Move volume, push markup gradually as relationships max.
  • Empty dealers. The #1 silent income leak. Bricks, daily, no exceptions.
  • Unpaid or bedless employees. They don't complain; they just stop. Audit the briefcase on every 4 AM round.
  • Ignoring the laundering caps. If your dirty pile grows week over week, buy the next business or raise deposits — dirty money is dead weight.
  • Cartel missteps: graffiti before hostile (wasted), unlocking all regional customers early (dealer farm goes dormant), forgetting the truce blocks progression past Westville. All covered in Phase VI — the only phase worth re-reading twice.
  • Trash buildup on long saves. Hyland Point's litter accumulates forever and drags performance. A Cleaner handles your properties; for the town itself, trash runs help, and the console's cleartrash command exists as a repair tool (back up your save before any console use).
  • Skipping drying racks. Dried = a full quality tier = +15% price per tier above customer standards. Racks pay for themselves in days.
  • Expanding production past demand. Sixteen mixed-coke pots satisfy the whole town. Spend surplus on the war and net worth, not pot #80.
  • Early Access drift. Monthly patches move prices, timings, and spawn rules. When a number here disagrees with your game, trust your game and keep the strategy.

That's the whole playbook. Follow the phases, respect the warnings, feed the dealers, and Hyland Point is yours.
